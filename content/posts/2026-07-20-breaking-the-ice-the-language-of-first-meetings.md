## Today's Idiom

> **break the ice**

This session is built around the classic idiom 'break the ice' and the vocabulary of meeting people for the first time. Learners explored how English speakers ease awkward moments, build connections, and describe their social comfort levels. Two related expressions round out the picture of how friendships and rapport develop.

## 💬 Idioms

### “break the ice”

To say or do something that helps people relax and start talking in an awkward or unfamiliar social situation.

- *The teacher played a name game to break the ice on the first day of class.*
- *He broke the ice by complimenting her on her presentation.*

### “hit it off”

To immediately like each other and get along well, usually from the very first meeting.

- *My sister and her new roommate hit it off right away.*
- *We hit it off at the conference and have stayed in touch ever since.*

### “warm up to someone”

To gradually become more comfortable and friendly with someone over time — not instantly, but slowly.

- *It took a while, but the team slowly warmed up to the new manager.*
- *My cat doesn't warm up to strangers easily, but she likes you.*

## 📚 Vocabulary to Remember

- **icebreaker** — An activity, game, or question designed to help people relax and get to know each other at the start of a meeting or event.
  - *The workshop facilitator used a two-truths-and-a-lie icebreaker to get everyone talking.*
- **small talk** — Light, casual conversation about everyday topics like the weather or the weekend — not deep or serious discussion.
  - *While waiting for the meeting to start, we made small talk about the long weekend.*
- **rapport** — A warm, trusting relationship in which people communicate easily and feel comfortable with each other.
  - *The doctor quickly built rapport with her patient by listening carefully and without rushing.*
- **approachable** — Friendly and easy to talk to; giving the impression that you welcome conversation.
  - *He always smiles and makes eye contact, which makes him seem very approachable.*
- **self-conscious** — Uncomfortably aware of yourself and worried that others are judging or watching you.
  - *She felt self-conscious walking into the party alone.*
- **awkward** — Causing or feeling social discomfort or embarrassment; not relaxed or smooth.
  - *There was an awkward silence after he said the wrong name.*
- **mingle** — To move around and talk to different people at a social gathering.
  - *The host encouraged the guests to mingle before dinner was served.*
- **introvert** — A person who feels more energized by time alone than by social situations and may find large groups tiring.
  - *As an introvert, she preferred one-on-one conversations to crowded parties.*

## 🔧 Say It Better

1. ❌ *She breaked the ice by telling a funny story.*
   ✅ **She broke the ice by telling a funny story.**
   💡 'Break' is irregular: break → broke → broken. Never add '-ed'.
2. ❌ *We need to break an ice before the presentation.*
   ✅ **We need to break the ice before the presentation.**
   💡 'Break the ice' is a fixed idiom — the article is always 'the', never 'an'.
3. ❌ *He does a lot of small talks at parties.*
   ✅ **He makes a lot of small talk at parties.**
   💡 'Small talk' is uncountable — no plural 's'. The natural verb is 'make', not 'do'.
4. ❌ *I always feel very self-conscience when I meet new people.*
   ✅ **I always feel very self-conscious when I meet new people.**
   💡 The adjective is 'self-conscious' (aware of being watched); 'conscience' is a different word meaning moral sense of right and wrong.
5. ❌ *We hitted it off the moment we met.*
   ✅ **We hit it off the moment we met.**
   💡 'Hit' is an irregular verb that never changes form: hit → hit → hit. Never 'hitted'.
6. ❌ *My new colleague is very approach and easy to talk to.*
   ✅ **My new colleague is very approachable and easy to talk to.**
   💡 You need the adjective form 'approachable' (suffix -able); 'approach' alone is a verb or noun.
7. ❌ *After few weeks, I finally warmed up with my new neighbors.*
   ✅ **After a few weeks, I finally warmed up to my new neighbors.**
   💡 Two errors: 'a few' requires the article 'a', and the correct preposition in this idiom is 'to', not 'with'.

## ✅ Check Yourself

**Q1.** The trainer asked everyone to share one surprising fact about themselves to ____ before the seminar began.

- break the ice
- hit it off
- warm up to someone

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>break the ice</strong> — 'Break the ice' describes a deliberate action to ease tension at the start of a group event; 'hit it off' is a result, not an action; 'warm up to someone' is a gradual personal process, not a group activity.</p></details>

**Q2.** The two neighbors ran into each other at the grocery store and immediately ____.

- hit it off
- broke the ice
- mingled

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>hit it off</strong> — 'Hit it off' means to instantly like each other; 'broke the ice' means to ease awkwardness in one moment, not to build a connection; 'mingled' means to socialize broadly in a group, not with one specific person.</p></details>

**Q3.** A well-chosen ____ at the start of a team meeting helps everyone relax and feel ready to contribute.

- icebreaker
- introvert
- rapport

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>icebreaker</strong> — An 'icebreaker' is a specific activity or question used to ease tension in a group; 'rapport' is a relationship quality; 'introvert' is a personality type, not an activity.</p></details>

**Q4.** She kept glancing around the room, sure that everyone was staring at her — she always felt ____ in large groups.

- self-conscious
- approachable
- awkward

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>self-conscious</strong> — The clue 'sure that everyone was staring at her' points to 'self-conscious', which means uncomfortably aware of being watched; 'approachable' is a positive, outward-facing quality; 'awkward' describes a situation or behavior rather than a private feeling of being observed.</p></details>

**Q5.** It took several weeks for the new hire to ____ her reserved desk neighbor.

- warmed up to
- mingled with
- broke the ice with

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>warmed up to</strong> — 'Warm up to' describes a gradual process of becoming comfortable with someone over time; 'broke the ice with' refers to one opening moment; 'mingled with' means to socialize in a group setting.</p></details>
