## 💬 Idioms

### “get down to business”

to stop chatting or delaying and start focusing on the real task at hand

- *We only have an hour for this meeting, so let's get down to business.*
- *The teacher told the students to stop talking and get down to business.*

### “ballpark figure”

a rough, approximate estimate rather than an exact number

- *I don't have the final cost yet, but a ballpark figure would be around three thousand dollars.*
- *Can you give me a ballpark figure for how long the renovation will take?*

### “red tape”

excessive bureaucratic rules, paperwork, or procedures that slow progress down

- *We wanted to open a small café, but there was so much red tape that it took a year just to get the permits.*
- *Starting a business abroad can be frustrating because of all the red tape involved.*

### “not my cup of tea”

something you do not enjoy or have no interest in

- *Managing a spreadsheet every day is not my cup of tea — I much prefer talking to people.*
- *She said that owning a business was not her cup of tea, but she deeply admired those who tried.*

## 📚 Vocabulary to Remember

- **savory** — a flavor that is salty or meaty — the opposite of sweet
  - *I ordered a savory crepe filled with ham and cheese instead of the sweet chocolate one.*
- **mock interview** — a practice job interview done before the real one, usually with a coach or a friend
  - *Doing a mock interview with her mentor helped her feel calm on the actual interview day.*
- **refurbish** — to clean, repair, or improve an old object so it looks or works like new again
  - *He bought a damaged wooden chair at a thrift store and refurbished it over the weekend.*
- **resell** — to sell something you have bought, usually at a higher price, in order to make a profit
  - *She finds branded clothes at garage sales and resells them online for a higher price.*
- **business-minded** — naturally good at thinking about profit, strategy, and how to run a business
  - *Even as a student, he was very business-minded and was always looking for ways to earn extra money.*
- **incremental** — happening in small, steady steps rather than all at once
  - *The company focused on incremental growth each year instead of trying to expand too fast.*
- **startup** — a newly created business, often in technology, built around a new idea or product
  - *Most of her classmates had ideas for a startup, but she preferred to work for an established company first.*
- **dress code** — a set of rules about what clothing is acceptable in a particular place or situation
  - *The office has a relaxed dress code, so most people wear jeans and casual shirts.*

## 🔧 Say It Better

1. ❌ *I learns how to wax floors.*
   ✅ **I learned how to wax floors.**
   💡 Use simple past 'learned' for a completed action; 'learns' is third-person present singular and doesn't match 'I.'
2. ❌ *You don't have to be a rocket science.*
   ✅ **You don't have to be a rocket scientist.**
   💡 The fixed expression refers to the person ('rocket scientist'), not the field ('rocket science').
3. ❌ *We had to do a lot of kind of bread tape.*
   ✅ **We had to deal with a lot of red tape.**
   💡 The idiom is 'red tape' (bureaucratic obstacles), not 'bread tape'; 'deal with' is the natural verb to pair with it.
4. ❌ *I firstly made a new site for TikTok time.*
   ✅ **I recently created a new website for Talk Time.**
   💡 'Firstly' introduces the first item in a list; use 'recently' or 'just' to mean 'not long ago.'
5. ❌ *I think maybe pass the 1st round.*
   ✅ **I think I may have passed the first round.**
   💡 The subject 'I' is missing, and 'may have passed' is needed to express uncertainty about something that happened in the past.
6. ❌ *It was surprised to me that all my classmates had startup ideas.*
   ✅ **I was surprised to find that all my classmates had startup ideas.**
   💡 The person feels the emotion, not the situation: say 'I was surprised,' not 'it was surprised to me.'
7. ❌ *Every person who have a business, he is a hero, for my opinion.*
   ✅ **Every person who has a business is a hero, in my opinion.**
   💡 Use 'has' after 'who' (singular); the subject already covers everyone, so no extra 'he' is needed; say 'in my opinion,' not 'for my opinion.'
8. ❌ *I do be late today.*
   ✅ **I was late today.**
   💡 'Do be' is not used in standard English for simple statements; the simple past 'was' is correct here.
9. ❌ *My imaginal crepe is sweet one.*
   ✅ **The crepe I had imagined was a sweet one.**
   💡 'Imaginal' is not a standard English word; use a relative clause like 'the crepe I had imagined' instead.
10. ❌ *I apply for some other jobs like cashier or retail associate.*
   ✅ **I'm applying for some other jobs, like cashier or retail associate.**
   💡 Use the present progressive ('I'm applying') for an action that is in progress right now, not the simple present.
