## Session Overview

The class explored US coins and bills, focusing on how Americans write and say numbers — especially the difference between using a period as a decimal mark versus a comma as a thousands separator. Learners then compared payment habits in their home countries, discussing tap to pay, QR codes, Venmo, and even government-backed digital currencies. The session ended with a creative shop-building activity to practice writing prices and a round of the vocabulary card game Apples to Apples.

## 💬 Idioms

### “apples to apples”

Comparing two things that are alike and in the same category; the opposite, 'apples to oranges,' means comparing things that are too different to be fairly compared

- *Before choosing a phone plan, compare apples to apples — make sure both options include the same data limit.*
- *Saying a bicycle is slower than a plane is apples to oranges; they serve completely different purposes.*

### “bad apple”

One dishonest or troublesome person in an otherwise good group, who risks negatively influencing the others

- *Most of the staff is reliable, but one bad apple keeps calling in sick on busy weekends.*
- *The coach said one bad apple on the team was spreading a negative attitude before every game.*

### “hardly worth anything”

Having almost no monetary or practical value; not worth the cost or effort

- *The US penny is hardly worth anything now — it costs more to produce than its face value.*
- *After sitting in the garage for fifteen years, the old exercise bike was hardly worth anything.*

## 📚 Vocabulary to Remember

- **denomination** — The official face value printed on a coin or banknote, such as $1, $5, or $20
  - *The cashier asked if I wanted my change in small denominations so I could use it at the parking meter.*
- **counterfeit** — Fake money (or goods) made to look like the real thing in order to deceive people
  - *The store uses a special pen to check whether large bills are counterfeit before accepting them.*
- **decimal mark** — The punctuation symbol that separates a whole number from its fractional part; in the US, a period (.) is used, so $3.75 means three dollars and seventy-five cents
  - *In the US, the decimal mark is a period, so 3.75 means three dollars and seventy-five cents, not three thousand and seventy-five.*
- **in circulation** — Actively being used and passed around in the economy; not stored away or taken out of use
  - *The US Mint stopped making new pennies, so only the coins already in circulation will remain.*
- **cover fee** — An entrance charge you pay at the door of a bar, club, or event, separate from anything you buy inside
  - *The venue charges a $10 cover fee on Friday nights, so it is a good idea to bring cash.*
- **tap to pay** — A contactless payment method where you hold your phone or credit card near a reader to complete a purchase without swiping or inserting a card
  - *I left my wallet at home, but I was still able to use tap to pay with my phone to buy lunch.*
- **enunciate** — To pronounce each word clearly and distinctly so the listener can easily understand
  - *Language teachers tend to enunciate more carefully in class than they do in everyday conversation.*
- **heirloom** — A valuable or meaningful object that has been passed down from one generation to the next within a family
  - *My grandmother's gold watch is a family heirloom that will one day belong to my daughter.*

## 🔧 Say It Better

1. ❌ *She don't know who this is.*
   ✅ **She doesn't know who this is.**
   💡 Third-person singular subjects (he / she / it) require 'doesn't,' not 'don't.'
2. ❌ *I have one year here and I never use any of the cash.*
   ✅ **I have been here for one year and I have never used any cash.**
   💡 Use the present perfect ('have been,' 'have used') for situations that started in the past and continue up to now.
3. ❌ *My wife don't like to meet Korean people.*
   ✅ **My wife doesn't like to meet Korean people.**
   💡 Third-person singular subjects need 'doesn't' in negative sentences.
4. ❌ *But didn't use any Korean languaging.*
   ✅ **But they didn't use any Korean.**
   💡 'Languaging' is not a standard English word; the sentence also needs a subject ('they') to be complete.
5. ❌ *And the 1st time, I cannot catch it.*
   ✅ **The first time, I couldn't catch it.**
   💡 Use 'couldn't' (past tense of 'can't') when describing a past inability; 'cannot' is present tense.
6. ❌ *It's very difficult to understand native speakers, speed, speaking speed.*
   ✅ **It is very difficult to understand native speakers' speaking speed.**
   💡 Use the possessive apostrophe ('speakers'') to show that the speed belongs to the speakers.
7. ❌ *Sometimes I'm eating the word, I think, omitting the word.*
   ✅ **Sometimes I think I'm dropping or omitting words.**
   💡 'Eating a word' is not standard English; 'dropping a word' or 'omitting a word' expresses the idea naturally.
8. ❌ *There is some other means to paint with the coin.*
   ✅ **There are some other ways to pay with currency.**
   💡 'Means' used as a plural noun takes 'are'; 'paint' appears to be a mishearing of 'pay'; 'currency' is clearer than 'coin' here.
9. ❌ *In my phone, one app downloaded, they pay this app for money.*
   ✅ **On my phone, I downloaded an app that I use to pay.**
   💡 Use 'on' with devices ('on my phone'); the sentence needs a clear subject ('I') and a relative clause to connect the ideas.
10. ❌ *Sometime I bought Facebook Marketplace, sometimes people says only he accepted cash.*
   ✅ **Sometimes on Facebook Marketplace, people say they only accept cash.**
   💡 'Sometimes' (not 'sometime') for repeated events; 'people say' needs no -s; use 'they' not 'he' when referring back to 'people.'

Near the exit, a jewellery stall caught my eye; the maker explained that each piece was crafted to become a family **heirloom**, designed to be worn and passed on for generations. I spent more than I planned, but I walked home with a full bag and no regrets.
