## Session Overview

The class opened with a moral dilemma about choosing between two bad options, introducing the idiom 'between a rock and a hard place.' Learners then shared personal stories about driving as teenagers in Mexico, exploring Austin's parks, and kayaking on Lake Travis, comparing outdoor culture and nighttime safety across South Korea, China, and the United States. One learner described a month-long solo Amtrak journey from San Diego through Chicago and back to Austin, sparking discussion about train passes, senior discounts, and spontaneous travel.

## 💬 Idioms

### “between a rock and a hard place”

Stuck in a situation where every available choice leads to a bad result; forced to pick between two difficult options.

- *She was between a rock and a hard place: cancel the flight and lose the money, or travel while sick.*
- *The small restaurant was between a rock and a hard place — raise prices and lose regulars, or keep prices and go broke.*

### “on and off”

Not continuously; sometimes happening and sometimes not, in an irregular or inconsistent pattern.

- *We have been talking about moving to a bigger apartment on and off for two years without ever deciding.*
- *The internet connection was on and off all morning, so the video call kept cutting out.*

### “bad apple”

One person in a group who causes trouble or has a negative effect on everyone else — from the idea that a single rotten apple can spoil an entire barrel.

- *Most of the students were engaged and polite; there was just one bad apple who kept interrupting.*
- *The coach reminded everyone before the tournament: 'We are a team — don't be the bad apple.'*

## 📚 Vocabulary to Remember

- **consequence** — A result or effect — often negative — that follows from a decision or action.
  - *Ignoring a small car problem now can have serious consequences later.*
- **conditions** — The specific rules or requirements that must be followed as part of an agreement or offer.
  - *Before signing the lease, read all the conditions so you know what is expected of you.*
- **within** — Before a time limit ends; inside a specified period.
  - *You must use the promotional code within 14 days of receiving it.*
- **shade** — An area protected from direct sunlight, making it feel cooler.
  - *On a hot day, the children moved their game to the shade of a large tree.*
- **humid** — Describing air that contains a high level of moisture, making hot weather feel even more uncomfortable.
  - *August in Austin is extremely humid, so even a short walk outside leaves you sweating.*
- **register** — To officially record something — a vehicle, a product, or yourself — with an authority or organization.
  - *You need to register your boat before you can legally launch it on a public lake.*
- **paddle** — A flat-bladed pole used to propel a small boat through water; also a verb meaning to move a boat this way.
  - *We took turns to paddle the canoe upstream against the slow current.*
- **debt** — An amount of money that you owe to another person or organization.
  - *She worked extra shifts every weekend to pay off her credit-card debt.*

## 🔧 Say It Better

1. ❌ *We used to kayaking.*
   ✅ **We used to go kayaking.**
   💡 'Used to' must be followed by a base verb, not a gerund (-ing form). The correct pattern is 'used to go kayaking.'
2. ❌ *In the May, I go there.*
   ✅ **In May, I went there.**
   💡 Month names never take the article 'the.' Also use past tense ('went') for a completed past action.
3. ❌ *Some places is permitted to kayak.*
   ✅ **Kayaking is permitted in some places.**
   💡 The grammatical subject should be the activity ('kayaking'), not 'places.' Add the preposition 'in' before 'some places.'
4. ❌ *I also traveled it by myself.*
   ✅ **I also traveled by myself.**
   💡 'Travel' is intransitive and cannot take a direct object — simply remove 'it.'
5. ❌ *So I'm not have the 30 days.*
   ✅ **So I didn't have the full 30 days.**
   💡 Past tense negation requires 'didn't + base verb.' The form 'I'm not have' incorrectly mixes present and past structures.
6. ❌ *The last month I go to the California, the seaside.*
   ✅ **Last month I went to California, to the seaside.**
   💡 Time expressions like 'last month' take no article. State and country names take no 'the' (with a few exceptions). Use past tense for a completed trip.
7. ❌ *At 15, but with a sign letter of your parents.*
   ✅ **At 15, but with a signed letter from your parents.**
   💡 'Signed' (past participle as adjective) means the letter carries a signature. Also use 'from,' not 'of,' when indicating the source of a document.
8. ❌ *Because of the heart, in the summer.*
   ✅ **Because of the heat in the summer.**
   💡 'Heat' (high temperature) and 'heart' (the organ) sound very similar. When talking about uncomfortable weather, the word you need is 'heat.'
9. ❌ *He's clean a rock and a hard place.*
   ✅ **It's a real rock-and-a-hard-place situation.**
   💡 The full idiom is 'between a rock and a hard place.' The key preposition is 'between,' and the whole phrase is fixed — avoid substituting or rearranging its words.
10. ❌ *My parents, authorization, or concept, or... 15 is possible in Mexico.*
   ✅ **With my parents' consent — or authorization — you can get a license at 15 in Mexico.**
   💡 'Consent' means agreement or permission; 'concept' means an abstract idea. They sound similar but have very different meanings — for permission, use 'consent' or 'authorization.'

## ✅ Check Yourself

**Q1.** My boss needs me to work this Saturday, but I already promised my children a day out — I'm really ____.

- between a rock and a hard place
- on and off
- bad apple

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>between a rock and a hard place</strong> — Both choices lead to a bad outcome (disappointing the boss or the children), which is exactly what 'between a rock and a hard place' describes. 'On and off' describes frequency and 'bad apple' describes a troublesome person — neither fits a dilemma.</p></details>

**Q2.** She practices piano ____, sometimes every day and sometimes not at all, so her progress is slow.

- on and off
- within
- bad apple

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>on and off</strong> — 'On and off' describes an irregular, inconsistent pattern over time. 'Within' sets a time deadline and 'bad apple' refers to a troublesome person — neither fits the idea of inconsistent practice.</p></details>

**Q3.** If your kayak has a motor, you are legally required to ____ it with the state water authority before launching.

- register
- paddle
- shade

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>register</strong> — 'Register' means to officially record a vehicle with an authority. 'Paddle' is a movement verb and 'shade' refers to shelter from sunlight — neither belongs in a legal registration sentence.</p></details>

**Q4.** Please review all the ____ of the membership before you pay, especially the cancellation policy.

- conditions
- debt
- consequence

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>conditions</strong> — 'Conditions' are the specific rules or terms of an agreement. 'Debt' is money owed and 'consequence' is the result of an action — neither describes the terms of a membership plan.</p></details>

**Q5.** All applications must be submitted ____ 30 days of the original purchase date.

- within
- humid
- on and off

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>within</strong> — 'Within' introduces a time boundary ('before 30 days are up'). 'Humid' is a weather adjective and 'on and off' describes frequency — neither fits a deadline sentence.</p></details>

**Q6.** He borrowed money every month for a year and is now struggling to repay his ____.

- debt
- shade
- consequence

<details><summary>Show answer</summary><p><strong>debt</strong> — 'Debt' is the money owed after borrowing. You 'repay' a debt; you cannot 'repay shade' (shelter from sun) or 'repay a consequence' (a result) — those combinations make no sense.</p></details>

## ✍️ Mini Diary

> Last Saturday I woke up early and headed to a riverside park to beat the **humid** summer heat before the crowds arrived. I found a bench under some old oak trees where the **shade** made the morning feel almost comfortable, and I sat watching a family carry their kayak down to the water's edge. For a moment I was **between a rock and a hard place**: I desperately wanted to join them on the water, but I hadn't **registered** my own kayak with the city yet and didn't want to risk a fine. I decided to simply walk along the bank instead, listening to the soft sound of paddles pushing through the river. It turned out to be exactly the quiet morning I needed.
